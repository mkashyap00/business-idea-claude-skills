Framework for Business Model Viability and Structural Defensibility
This rubric evaluates whether the economic architecture of a business can endure competitive pressure and scale efficiently
.
1. The New Five Forces of Industry Structure Evaluate the industry's profitability and defensibility by auditing these competitive forces:
- Threat of New Entrants: Assess barriers to entry, such as economies of scale, initial capital requirements, and AI operational leverage (the ability of a new entrant to use automation for lower margins)
- Bargaining Power of Suppliers: Evaluate supply chain fragility. Are critical inputs or APIs controlled by a single source, or is the supply chain distributed?
- Bargaining Power of Buyers: Differentiate between B2B and B2C dynamics. B2B deals are justified through hard ROI models and committee decisions, while B2C relies on emotion, convenience, and shorter sales cycles
- Threat of Substitutes: Identify if a technological leap could replace the entire industry, or if the business possesses a proprietary data moat to defend itself. Rivalry Among Competitors: Analyze the Total Addressable Market (TAM), growth rate, and the number of existing competitors
- Macro Forces: Account for the new forces confronting businesses today, including technological inflection points, climate and environmental regulations, societal shifts, and geopolitical volatility.
2. Unit Economics and Scalability Triage A scalable business decouples inputs from outputs, growing revenue exponentially while costs increase marginally. Evaluate the following metrics:
- LTV to CAC Ratio: The Lifetime Value (LTV) of a customer should be at least 3 times the Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) for early-stage companies, moving toward 5:1 as they mature. 
- CAC Payback Period: The time required to recover acquisition costs should ideally be under 12 to 18 months. 
- Burn Multiple: This metric should be below 1.5 to indicate capital efficiency (spending less than $1.50 to generate $1 of new revenue)
- Rule of 40/50: For software companies, the combined revenue growth percentage and profit margin percentage should exceed 40% to 50%.
- Profit Margins: Audit the Gross Profit Margin (revenue minus cost of goods sold) and Net Profit Margin (profitability after all expenses).
